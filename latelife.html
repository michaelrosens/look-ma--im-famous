In 1837, Jackson retired to the Hermitage outside of Nashville, but he remained an influence in national politics. He abandoned his caution regarding Texas to become a vocal advocate for its annexation and was instrumental in persuading fellow Tennessean James K. Polk to run for president in 1844.

During his final years, Jackson experienced the misery of constant infections, pain, and vision and hearing problems. While he frequently predicted his own death, he continued to fight and hang on to life for the better part of a decade. Finally, on June 8, 1845, surrounded by his loved ones, he died in his bedroom at The Hermitage.
